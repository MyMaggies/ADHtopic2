Introduction

Hook: The time a story is written can really shape the characters and their problems.

Background: Henrik Ibsen's A Doll's House is set in 19th-century Europe, when society had very strict rules for women and they had few chances in life.

Thesis: Ibsen's A Doll's House clearly shows the limiting social rules for women in the 1800s, rules that Nora Helmer lives by at first, but eventually bravely fights against.

Body Paragraph 1: How Society Limited Women

  • Point: In the 19th century, society had very specific expectations for how women should behave, especially in marriage.
  • Evidence:
    • Torvald constantly uses belittling names for Nora like "my little skylark" and "my pretty little pet," showing that men saw women as less intelligent and dependent.
    • Society said women should be wives and mothers, take care of the home and kids, and not have much say in money or learning. (e.g., Mrs. Linde marrying for money because women had few other options.)
    • The laws at the time gave husbands control over their wives' money and legal matters, like Nora not being able to legally sign the loan.
  • Explanation: These examples show how little freedom women had because of society's rules. These rules shaped how Nora first saw her role and how much she relied on Torvald.
  • Link: These societal expectations explain why Nora acts the way she does at the beginning and set the stage for her later realization that she's being held back.

Body Paragraph 2: Living by Society's Rules (PEEL)

*Combine the following components into a complete paragraph (approx. 135 words):

Point

Nora’s initial characterization serves as a direct embodiment of 19th-century domestic constraints, illustrating how women were forced into performative and infantilized roles to survive within a patriarchal structure.

Evidence

This is evident through her obsession with superficial appearances, her concealment of trivial secrets, and her willing submission to Torvald’s judgment regarding complex issues like money.

Explanation

Ibsen highlights that her behavior is a direct result of how society taught her to be. She prioritizes the "perfect wife" role over her own autonomy, masking her intelligence.

Link

Consequently, Nora living by these rules creates the main conflict of the play, making her eventual awakening and rebellion against these exact constraints incredibly impactful.
